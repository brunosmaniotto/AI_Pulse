## SLIDE 2: The Promise and the Problem
**[0:30 -- 2:30]**

So, there's been enormous interest in using AI for research, and for good reason. Tools like ChatGPT, Claude, and Gemini are genuinely useful for certain tasks: brainstorming ideas, explaining complex concepts, writing and debugging code, summarizing long documents.

But for academic research specifically, there's a critical flaw that many people discover the hard way.

These tools can present completely fabricated information --- fake citations, nonexistent papers, invented statistics --- as though they were established fact. And they do it confidently, without any warning.

Today's workshop has three parts. First, we'll look at *why* this happens and how bad the problem actually is. Then we'll look at specialized tools designed to avoid it --- Perplexity, Consensus, and Elicit. And finally, we'll look at the frontier: autonomous research agents like Kosmos that don't just find papers, but actually analyze your data.

We'll do live demos throughout, so you can see these tools in action.

## SLIDE 4: The Hallucination Problem
**[3:00 -- 6:00]**

So why do large language models hallucinate? The fundamental issue is that these models are designed to predict *plausible* text, not *accurate* text. They don't have a database of facts. They have patterns learned from training data, and they generate whatever continuation seems most likely.

When it comes to citations, this is particularly dangerous. A fake citation like "Smith and Johnson, 2020" follows all the patterns the model has learned from millions of real papers --- common author surnames, recent year, proper formatting, a journal name that sounds right. The model generates text that *looks* like a real citation because it's structurally similar to real citations. But it was never verified against any database.

And the numbers on this are striking. A study by Walters and Wilder in *Scientific Reports* found that ChatGPT fabricates somewhere between 18 and 55 percent of its citations, depending on the model version. And even when it cites a real paper, 24 to 43 percent of those references contain substantive errors --- wrong authors, wrong year, wrong journal, or attributed findings that the paper never actually made.

It's not just general chatbots either. Magesh and colleagues tested specialized legal AI tools --- these are products marketed specifically for legal research --- and found hallucination rates between 17 and 33 percent of queries.

So this isn't a minor issue. It's pervasive, and it persists even in tools designed for professional use.

---

## SLIDE 5: Real-World Consequences
**[6:00 -- 8:30]**

And the consequences are real.

In consulting, Deloitte Australia published a welfare report in 2025 that contained fabricated references. The same year, Deloitte Canada published a healthcare report with fake citations. These are reports that inform government policy.

In law, we have the now-famous Mata v. Avianca case from 2023, where a lawyer cited six completely nonexistent cases generated by ChatGPT. A judge sanctioned the attorneys. In 2025, a California appeal was filed where 21 out of 23 quoted cases were fabricated. Hundreds of errant legal citations have been flagged across US courts at this point.

And in academia --- this is the one that should concern us most directly --- over 100 hallucinated citations were found across 51 accepted papers at NeurIPS 2025. These are papers that went through peer review at a top venue and still contained fake references. Once those fake citations get published, they get cited by other papers, and the problem cascades.

So this is the landscape. Now let's talk about what we can do about it.

## SLIDE 6: Why Specialized Tools?
**[8:30 -- 10:00]**

The key insight is the difference between *generation* and *retrieval*.

General AI tools --- ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini --- work by generating text from patterns. When you ask for a citation, the model generates something that looks like a citation. There's no database lookup, no verification step.

Academic AI tools --- Elicit, Consensus, Perplexity --- work fundamentally differently. They *retrieve* from verified sources. They're connected to databases of 200 million or more papers --- Semantic Scholar, OpenAlex, PubMed --- and when they cite something, it's because they found it in the database. The citation is real. You can click on it and read the paper.

That's the single most important distinction. Retrieval from verified sources versus generation from patterns.

## SLIDE 7: Three Tools, One Workflow
**[10:00 -- 11:30]**

The three tools we'll look at today --- Perplexity, Consensus, and Elicit --- all share a basic workflow: you ask a research question in natural language, you get back papers with AI-generated summaries, you can filter and explore, and you can export citations.

They all do semantic search, meaning they understand the meaning of your query rather than just matching keywords. They all provide sentence-level citations so you can verify claims. And they all let you export to reference managers.

Where they differ is in what they're best at. Perplexity is best for quick context and broad questions --- it searches academic papers *and* the web. Consensus is best for evidence-based questions --- "What does the evidence say about X?" --- and has a unique Consensus Meter feature. Elicit is best for systematic reviews and structured data extraction.

Let me show you each one, starting with Perplexity.

## SLIDE 8: Perplexity
**[11:30 -- 15:00]**

Perplexity's strength is breadth. It searches across academic papers, web sources, and news. It gives you numbered inline citations for every claim, so you can check the source.

Let me walk through the features, because there are some powerful ones that are easy to miss.

First, **model selection**. Perplexity lets you choose which AI model runs under the hood --- Claude, GPT, Grok, and others. This matters because different models have different strengths. If you're asking a reasoning-heavy question, you might pick one model; for a creative brainstorming query, another. You can experiment and see which gives you the best results for your field.

Second, **Academic Mode**. This restricts results to peer-reviewed sources only. If you're doing serious literature work, toggle this on --- it filters out blog posts, news articles, and other non-academic noise.

Third, **Deep Research**. This is a longer-running mode where Perplexity breaks your question into sub-questions, searches each one independently, and synthesizes across dozens of sources into a comprehensive report. Think of it as the difference between a quick Google search and spending an afternoon in the library.

And fourth, **Model Council**, which queries multiple models on the same question and synthesizes their answers. Useful when you want to cross-check.

## SLIDE 9: Perplexity Spaces
**[15:00 -- 17:30]**

So that's Perplexity for individual queries. But there's another feature that's particularly useful for students and research teams: **Spaces**.

A Space is a persistent, shareable research environment. Think of it like a shared folder, but one that you can have a conversation with.

Here's what you can do. You can **upload files** --- a syllabus, a set of readings, a dataset, a codebook, your notes. You can **invite collaborators** --- classmates, lab members, your advisor. And then anyone in the Space can **ask questions** that are grounded in both the uploaded materials *and* Perplexity's full web and academic search. Everything gets saved as a shared research trail that everyone can see and build on.

Let me give you a concrete example of how you might use this.

Say you're working on a final project with a partner. You create a Space, call it "Econ 101 Final Project." You upload your assigned readings --- maybe five or six papers --- plus your dataset. You invite your partner. Now, both of you can go in and ask questions like, "Which of our readings discusses instrumental variables?" or "Based on the data dictionary, which variable should be our dependent variable?" Perplexity answers using your uploaded materials as context, and both of you can see the full conversation history.

You're building a shared evidence base instead of each person searching independently and then trying to reconcile what you found on your own.

This is also useful for lab groups. Upload a set of key papers in your field, invite the whole lab, and use the Space as a living knowledge base that anyone can query.

It also lets you import your own library from Zotero and upload papers directly. And Berkeley has a premium license, so you get full access with your Berkeley email.

## SLIDE 11: Elicit
**[21:00 -- 24:30]**

Elicit is built for a different use case: systematic, structured research. If Perplexity is for broad exploration and Consensus is for gauging evidence, Elicit is for building a structured evidence base.

It has several workflows. **Extract Data** lets you build custom tables from papers --- you define the columns you want (sample size, effect size, methodology, population studied) and Elicit extracts those values from each paper. This is the kind of work that takes weeks in a systematic review, and Elicit can do a first pass in minutes.

**Competitive Landscape** is useful for comparing approaches or products across both academic and non-academic sources. And their **General Research Agent** is a flexible mode that breaks down any research question into sub-tasks.

**[DEMO]**

*[Navigate to Elicit. Type the query. Wait for results.]*

Notice how Elicit immediately gives you a structured table. Each row is a paper, and the columns show key extracted information. I can add custom columns --- let me add "sample size" and "country" --- and Elicit will read the papers and populate those fields.

This is enormously powerful for anyone doing a meta-analysis or systematic review. You still need to verify the extractions, but as a first pass, it saves a huge amount of time.

The free tier gives you a reasonable number of queries per month. For heavy systematic review work, they have paid plans.

## SLIDE 12: When to Use Something Else
**[24:30 -- 27:00]**

OK, so I've shown you three powerful tools. Now let me be honest about when they're *not* the right choice.

**First, these tools are slower than ChatGPT.** And that's by design. The reason they're slower is that they're actually searching databases and verifying sources instead of just generating text. More verification takes more time. So if you just need to brainstorm ideas or draft an outline, ChatGPT or Claude is still the faster option. Use these tools when you need *sourced, verifiable* information.

**Second, there are real coverage gaps.** Very recent papers --- anything published in the last three months or so --- may not be indexed yet. These databases need time to ingest new publications. If you're tracking a fast-moving field and need the latest preprints from last week, you might not find them here.

Niche topics with thin literature are another gap. If your research question is so specialized that only a handful of papers exist, these tools may not surface them as well as a carefully constructed Boolean search in a specialized database.

And non-English publications. These tools are heavily biased toward English-language literature. If your field has important work published in other languages, these tools will miss it.

**So when should you still use Google Scholar or your library's database?** When you need exhaustive coverage --- dissertations, theses, grey literature, working papers. When you already have a title or DOI and just need to find it --- a known-item search. When you need full-text access through Berkeley's subscriptions. And when you need non-English or region-specific literature.

The best approach is to use these tools to get started and build your initial evidence base, then fill the gaps with traditional database searches. They complement each other.

## SLIDE 13: Discussion
**[27:00 -- 30:30]**

So we've seen what these tools can do and where they fall short. Before we move to the frontier, I want to pause and put five questions on the table. These are things I think about a lot, and I'd love to hear your reactions.

**First: teach or restrict?** Students are going to use these tools. Other researchers are going to use these tools. That ship has sailed. So the question is --- are we better off trying to police and restrict their use, or are we better off teaching people how to use them well? And if we teach them, what does "using them well" actually mean? What does responsible use look like in a seminar paper versus a dissertation versus a grant proposal?

*[Pause for 15--20 seconds. Take 1--2 audience responses if offered.]*

**Second: industry is pushing AI.** This isn't a case where industry is passively allowing AI. Companies are actively requiring AI fluency. Consulting firms expect you to use these tools. Tech companies assume it. So if you're training students for careers outside academia --- and most of us are --- how does that change what skills we prioritize? Do we spend less time on manual literature searches and more time on prompt engineering and verification? What do we drop to make room?

**Third --- and this is the one I find most interesting --- think about the arc of information.** In the 20th century, information was low volume, high signal-to-noise. Card catalogs, reference librarians, interlibrary loans --- access was the bottleneck, but you could largely trust what you found. Published meant vetted.

Then Google came along, and we moved to high volume, low signal-to-noise. It wasn't about *finding* information anymore --- it was about *evaluating* it. Knowing how to search, how to assess sources, how to distinguish reliable from unreliable.

Now we're in another shift. AI gives us on-demand volume with an *unobservable* signal-to-noise ratio. The information looks just as polished whether it's real or fabricated. You can't tell from the surface anymore. So what's the bottleneck now? And here's the real question: we can't imagine doing research without Google today. What will research look like in 25 years? What skills will matter then that we're not teaching now?

*[Pause. Let this land. Take a response or two if the room engages.]*

**Fourth: equity and access.** These tools have free tiers, which is great. But the premium features --- Deep Research, unlimited queries, larger Spaces --- cost money. Kosmos is free for .edu emails, but what about researchers at institutions that don't have those partnerships? Does AI narrow the gap between well-funded and under-funded researchers, because the free tiers are so powerful? Or does it widen the gap, because the premium tiers are where the real advantage is?

**And fifth: verification as a core skill.** We started this workshop talking about hallucination. These specialized tools reduce it dramatically, but they don't eliminate it. And as AI gets better at generating plausible-sounding research, the ability to *verify* --- to check whether a claim is actually supported, whether a citation actually says what someone claims it says --- might become the single most important skill in academia. Is that something we're teaching explicitly? Should we be?

I don't have clean answers to any of these. But I think they're worth sitting with as we look at the next generation of tools.

## SLIDE 15: What is Kosmos?
**[31:30 -- 33:00]**

Now, everything we've looked at so far helps you *find* and *summarize* papers. Kosmos does something fundamentally different. It's an autonomous research agent.

Kosmos comes from FutureHouse, now Edison Scientific. You give it a research question and a dataset --- up to 15 gigabytes --- and it goes to work. It reads hundreds or thousands of papers, writes and executes code to analyze your data, iterates on its approach, and delivers a full research report. A typical run takes about 12 hours.

It has two modes. The full Kosmos mode, which is the autonomous pipeline I just described. And interactive agents for more targeted tasks: literature synthesis, data analysis, molecular design, and precedent search.

For access, it's free for academics with a .edu email. You get 650 credits per month, which is roughly enough for several full research runs.

## SLIDE 16: Kosmos: Discoveries
**[33:00 -- 34:00]**

Is it actually good? A paper by Mitchener and colleagues on arXiv documents seven genuine discoveries that Kosmos made across four fields --- estimated at about six months of equivalent human research per run.

Some highlights. Kosmos used Mendelian randomization on GWAS and proteomics data to show that SOD2 causally reduces myocardial fibrosis in humans. It found that a protective GWAS variant regulates SSR1 expression in Type 2 diabetes. It identified the temporal ordering of extracellular matrix decline during tau accumulation in Alzheimer's disease.

It also independently reproduced known results in metabolomics, materials science, and connectomics --- important validation.

These are real, verifiable scientific findings. This isn't summarizing papers. This is doing science.

## SLIDE 17: Kosmos: Best Practices
**[34:00 -- 35:30]**

If you want to try Kosmos, here are the key best practices.

Give it a clear, feasible research objective --- something that would normally take weeks or months, not a single analysis. Provide enough context, as you would for an experienced colleague who just joined your lab. Describe your data clearly --- label columns intuitively or provide a codebook.

Complex data works best --- high-dimensional data like single-cell RNA-seq, proteomics, or multi-sample environmental data. Provide processed data, not raw files. And iterate --- start with a dataset you already know well, so you can evaluate the quality of its analysis.

## Q&A Buffer
**[36:30+]**

*Anticipated questions and talking points:*

- **"Which tool should I start with?"** --- It depends on your use case. Perplexity for broad exploration, Consensus for evidence-based questions, Elicit for systematic reviews. Try all three on the same question and see which output is most useful for your workflow.

- **"Can I trust the citations from these tools?"** --- The citations are real papers, but always verify the AI's *characterization* of what the paper says. The citation exists; the summary might still be imperfect.

- **"Is Kosmos free?"** --- Yes, for academics with a .edu email. You get 650 credits per month. A full research run costs around 100 credits depending on data size.

- **"How does this relate to ChatGPT/Claude?"** --- These tools are complementary. Use ChatGPT/Claude for brainstorming, coding, and drafting. Use the tools we covered today when you need grounded, verifiable academic information. The tools we showed today are slower --- that's the trade-off for verification.

- **"What about Perplexity Spaces --- is that free?"** --- You can create Spaces on the free tier with some limits. Pro gives you more storage and collaborator seats.

- **"What about the Kosmos experiment you mentioned?"** --- We're running an experiment testing whether Kosmos does genuine causal analysis or pattern-matches to known literature. We use the same dataset in five different framings and compare results. Happy to share findings when they're ready.

- **"On the equity question --- doesn't free access to these tools actually help?"** --- It's both. The free tiers are genuinely powerful and democratize access in ways that didn't exist before. But the gap between free and premium is real, and it maps onto existing institutional inequalities. Worth watching how this evolves.
