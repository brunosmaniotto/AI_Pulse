INTERVIEW TRANSCRIPT
Participant ID: 007 (Pseudonym: Maya)
Date: March 15, 2025
Duration: 45 minutes
Interview Setting: Student Center, private booth
Interviewer: Graduate researcher

---

INTERVIEWER: Thank you so much for taking the time to meet with me today. I know you're busy. So, we're talking about food access and experiences here at Berkeley. Can you start by telling me a bit about yourself and when you first noticed that food might be a concern for you?

MAYA: Um, yeah, of course. So I'm a junior, I'm majoring in environmental science, and I'm on financial aid—like, the maximum amount I can get. But it's still like, you know, it doesn't cover everything. I think the moment I really realized it was a problem was actually during fall of my sophomore year. I had just registered for this lab class that cost like $150 for the materials, and I also needed the textbook for my organic chem course, which was over $200. And I remember sitting at my laptop with my shopping cart, and I had to pick—like, literally, I could afford the textbook OR I could like, eat regularly that month. And that sounds dramatic when I say it out loud, but that's genuinely what I was calculating. So I... I ended up not buying the textbook, which was stressful, but um, I ate.

INTERVIEWER: How did you manage without the textbook?

MAYA: I borrowed it from the library—you know, you can only check it out for two hours at a time, which is annoying because you have to keep renewing. My roommate also had the class, so I'd coordinate with her. She'd read the chapters and we'd talk through them. But it was very... it was a scramble. And that's when I kind of realized, like, this is a real problem I'm gonna have to deal with for the rest of college if I don't figure something out. So yeah, that's kind of when it clicked for me.

INTERVIEWER: Okay, and once you realized this was going to be an ongoing issue, what were some of the ways you started coping with it? Like, what were your strategies?

MAYA: Oh man, um, I have so many coping strategies at this point. [laughs nervously] I've become like, really good at timing. So I learned pretty quickly that there are a ton of free food events on campus if you know where to look. Like, the environmental club—which I'm in—we have meetings on Thursdays, and there's always pizza. The engineering school does a lot of recruiting events with food. I've gotten to the point where I actually... I check the campus events calendar, and I'm like "okay, where's free food happening this week?" It's not ideal, you know, because it's not enough to live on, but it definitely helps fill in the gaps.

I also—and this is maybe not the healthiest thing—but I've become very strategic about eating at friends' houses. Like, if my friend invites me over or says "do you want to grab food?" I'm always yes, because that's like a guaranteed meal. Some of my friends know the situation, and they'll like, be proactive about inviting me over. And that's really nice, but it also makes me feel like... I don't know... like I'm maybe taking advantage of them? Even though they're like "no, of course, come over," there's still that guilt there.

I also work—I work about 15 hours a week at the library, and my meal plan—well, I had to choose between like a 10-meal-per-week plan and investing that money into groceries. So I did groceries, and I cook a lot. Rice, beans, eggs, whatever's cheapest. I meal-prep on Sundays.

INTERVIEWER: So you're balancing work, school, and all of that. How does the work situation play into this?

MAYA: It's tricky. Like, I need the money, obviously, because of the food and the textbooks and all of that. But 15 hours a week is a lot when you're taking four upper-level classes. I definitely have had semesters where I wanted to take on more hours for more money, but my GPA started slipping, and I was like, "okay, that's not worth it. I need to graduate." So it's like this constant negotiation, right? Do I work more and risk my grades, or do I work less and like, go hungry some weeks? I've had weeks where I definitely went hungry. Not starving, but like, I'd eat less intentionally so my groceries could stretch longer. It's weird to like, admit that.

INTERVIEWER: Yeah, that's a really difficult position to be in. And it sounds like there's a lot of stress around not wanting to tell people about this.

MAYA: [pauses] Yeah. Um, okay, so like, I go to Berkeley, right? And Berkeley is this bubble where everyone's kind of... wealthy, or at least, that's the vibe. Like, people don't really talk about money struggles. I have friends who like, complain about their parents not giving them money for spring break, and I'm just sitting there like, "I can't afford lunch." So I don't really... I don't tell people. I've told like, maybe two of my close friends, and I told them more by accident than on purpose. Like, I made a joke about being poor, and they took it seriously, and I had to explain.

But it's isolating. Like, there are times when my friends will be like "let's go out to eat!" and I have to make up an excuse. I can't say "I don't have money," because that's weird and awkward, so I'm like "oh, I'm not hungry," or "I have to study," when really I just can't afford it. And then I feel like they might think I don't want to hang out with them, you know? So there's this whole layer of social stuff that makes it harder.

INTERVIEWER: Have you ever had situations at restaurants or social eating situations that felt particularly difficult?

MAYA: Oh yeah. Okay, so one time—I must have been a freshman—my friends wanted to go to this restaurant, and I went because I didn't want to be like weird about it, and I just ordered like a side salad or something small. And one of my friends was like, "Why are you not getting an entree?" And I froze. I literally didn't know what to say. I couldn't just be like "because I can't afford it," so I was like "oh, I'm not that hungry," which made it even more awkward. And then everyone was like "get whatever you want, we'll cover you," which was kind of sweet, but also... I didn't want to be the person who needs to be covered. So I felt like I had to refuse, and then I felt bad, and the whole thing was just... uncomfortable. I haven't gone to sit-down restaurants with them very much since then.

INTERVIEWER: That's understandable. So it sounds like there's this disconnect between what you need and what you feel comfortable asking for or admitting to.

MAYA: Exactly. I feel like I should be fine, you know? Like, I got into Berkeley, I'm doing well in my classes, I'm not like, struggling academically. So the fact that I can't afford food feels like... it shouldn't be my problem. Like, maybe I'm not smart enough with my money, or maybe I should just not be here if I can't afford it. I know that's not rational, but that's how it feels sometimes. Like, there's shame in it.

INTERVIEWER: So let's talk about that shame a bit more, and also maybe shift to—have you found any resources that have helped?

MAYA: Yes! Actually, um, this is the thing that kind of made a big difference. So I think it was like late sophomore year when I found out there was a food pantry on campus. And I remember being terrified to go. Like, I built it up in my head as this scary thing where I'd run into someone I knew, or people would judge me, or like, it would feel bad. But I was also like, really hungry and out of money, so I went.

And it was... it was actually really good. The people who work there—I think they're mostly student workers and volunteers—they were so nice and not judgmental at all. They just like, helped me pick out stuff, and I got like, a whole bag of groceries. And I cried a little bit, which was embarrassing, but also like, it was such a relief. And I've been back, like, once a month or so since then? And it's gotten easier each time. Like, the shame is less.

But also—and I want to be clear about this—the pantry is amazing, but it also shows like, how inadequate the support system is for students like me. Like, why is a food pantry run by students even necessary? Why isn't food adequately covered by financial aid? I use the pantry, and I'm grateful, but I'm also kind of angry about it, you know? Like, the University is obviously aware that students can't afford food—they literally have a pantry—but they're not solving the actual problem.

INTERVIEWER: What would solving the actual problem look like to you?

MAYA: Um, honestly, I think the meal plans are too expensive. Like, a 10-meal-per-week plan is like $1,800 a semester, which is insane. And then you have dining dollars on top of that if you want to eat at any of the dining halls. And food is like, it's more expensive than it's ever been right now. So I think either the University needs to subsidize meal plans more, or they need to like, integrate food costs into financial aid more effectively. Like, financial aid should actually cover living expenses, not just tuition and books.

Also, I think more students need to know about the food pantry. Like, I didn't know about it until sophomore year, almost by accident. If there was like, better outreach, more students could use it. And maybe it wouldn't feel so isolated if more people were like, openly using it?

And I don't know, maybe this is idealistic, but I think universities should like, acknowledge that students are hungry. Like, normalize it, talk about it. Because right now there's so much shame, and the shame is actually like, a barrier to people asking for help.

INTERVIEWER: That's really insightful. I want to go back to something you mentioned earlier—you said a professor noticed something. Can you tell me about that?

MAYA: Oh, um, yeah. So I had Professor Chen for my ecology class in spring of my sophomore year, and I was doing okay in the class, but I think I was probably not as engaged as I could have been because I was like, stressed about money and food and all of that. And one day after class, Professor Chen like, pulled me aside and asked if everything was okay. And I was kind of surprised, because like, professors don't usually do that, you know?

And I kind of deflected at first, but she was really kind and persistent—not in a pushy way, but in like, a genuine way. And I ended up kind of venting about my situation. And she didn't like, solve my problems or give me money or anything, but she like, listened, and she made me feel like it wasn't shameful. She was like "this is a real problem, and you're not alone, and there are resources." And she actually connected me with a student organization that focuses on food justice and food access.

And that was like, huge for me. Because suddenly I wasn't isolated anymore. Like, I went to a meeting, and there were other students who had the exact same experience, and we like, talked about it openly. And it was such a relief to be like "oh, I'm not the only one. This isn't a personal failure." And then we started doing like, awareness campaigns and advocacy stuff, which made me feel like I was doing something about it instead of just like, suffering in silence.

INTERVIEWER: So that organization became a real source of support for you.

MAYA: It did, yeah. Like, the people in it became my friends too. So the social isolation part got better. And we're like, pushing the university on some of these issues now. We did a survey last semester about food insecurity, and we're trying to like, present the findings to administration. So there's actually a way to channel some of the frustration and anger into like, advocacy work, which feels better than just like, coping with it individually.

INTERVIEWER: And how has that felt, being part of that advocacy work?

MAYA: Empowering, honestly. Like, I'm still food insecure, right? Like, that hasn't changed. I still use the food pantry, I still have to be strategic about my meals. But feeling like I'm part of something that's trying to change the system rather than just like, surviving within it—that makes a huge difference to my mental health. Like, I feel less alone, I feel less ashamed, and I feel like maybe something I do now could help students coming after me.

Although, I will say, it's also frustrating because like, institutions move slowly, and the problem is urgent. Like, students are hungry now, and we're like, writing surveys and having meetings, and meanwhile people are going hungry. So there's also this feeling of like, am I doing enough? Should I be doing more?

INTERVIEWER: That's a really understandable tension. And it sounds like you've kind of moved from coping with this individually to thinking about it more systemically.

MAYA: Yeah, I think so. Like, I still do all the individual coping stuff—the meal prep, the strategic eating, all of that. But now I also think about it as like, a systemic issue, not a personal failure. And I think that's helped me feel less ashamed. Like, it's not because I'm bad at budgeting or because I'm not working hard enough. It's because the costs of college are not aligned with what students actually have.

INTERVIEWER: Is there anything else you want to share, or anything I haven't asked that you think is important?

MAYA: Um, I guess just like, validation that this is hard, and I think I sometimes feel like I should be "over it" by now, like I should have like, figured out the perfect system. But the truth is it's still stressful, it's still something I think about every single day, and I'm still sometimes hungry. And like, that's just the reality. And I think if the University understood that better, maybe they'd treat it as less of a charity issue and more of a justice issue.

Also, I want to say that like, the food pantry, and the student organization, and Professor Chen—those things matter. Like, people need to know that support exists and that it's okay to use it. Because I almost didn't, and that could have been really bad.

INTERVIEWER: Well, thank you so much for sharing all of this with me. I really appreciate your honesty and your willingness to like, go deep on this. This is really valuable for understanding what students are experiencing.

MAYA: Yeah, of course. I'm glad I could help. And like, I hope this actually leads to something, you know? Like, I hope this research doesn't just like, sit in a file somewhere. I hope it actually like, influences how the University thinks about these issues.

INTERVIEWER: That's the goal. Thank you again.
