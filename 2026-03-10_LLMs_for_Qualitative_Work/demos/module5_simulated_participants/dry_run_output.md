# Module 5: Simulating Participants and Pilot Testing
## UC Berkeley Campus Mental Health Services Survey - Dry Run Output

**Simulation Date:** February 25, 2026
**Tool:** Google Gemini 1.5 Pro
**Task:** Generate three contrasting personas responding to qualitative survey about campus mental health services
**Prompt Strategy:** Persona-specific system prompts with cultural identity and situational context

---

## Part 1: Jordan's Responses

**Participant Profile:** Jordan, 22, Computer Science PhD student, Asian American (Taiwanese immigrant family), first-generation college student

### Q1: Describe your experience trying to access mental health support at UC Berkeley.

I tried CAPS once during my first year of grad school when I was dealing with some imposter syndrome about my research. It was... fine? Very efficient, very procedural. I went in expecting a deep conversation, got a 50-minute intake that felt like going through a checklist. The therapist was competent but seemed to treat everyone the same way—like we were all coming in for the same thing. I didn't go back. Since then, I've mostly managed stress through running and late-night coding sessions, which honestly works better for me than talking did. It's interesting—I notice I process problems more clearly when I'm at the keyboard or on a 10K run than in a therapist's office. Haven't felt the need to try again, though I'm aware that's probably not a scalable solution long-term.

### Q2: What barriers, if any, have you encountered in getting the help you needed?

The main barrier is timing. CAPS has notoriously long wait times—when I finally called, I had to book like six weeks out, which kind of defeats the purpose when you're in crisis mode. But beyond logistics, there's this weird cultural thing. My parents—who came here from Taiwan when I was three—just don't talk about mental health. They see therapy as weakness, or worse, as something Americans do instead of handling it privately. So there's internal pressure not to seek help, even though I rationally know that's outdated. I'm aware enough to recognize that's a pattern I might eventually need to address, but it hasn't forced the issue yet. The other barrier is just fit—I don't think the standard clinical model matches how I work. I'm very head-focused, very rational. Sitting in a quiet room and talking about my feelings isn't where my brain naturally goes.

### Q3: How do you think the university could better support student mental health?

More on-demand options would help enormously. I'm not sure if it's a therapy model or an app or what, but students need flexibility. We're not operating on a nine-to-five schedule, so why would mental health support? I'd also push back on the assumption that therapy is the only solution. Some people need it, genuinely. But there could be more tech-enabled options—maybe AI chatbots for quick support, peer mentoring networks, or structured group activities around stress management that don't feel clinical. I realize I'm being a bit self-serving here since I work in tech, but the mental health infrastructure feels like it was designed for an earlier era. Also, I think CAPS is understaffed. Hire more people, especially people who can work flexible hours. That's probably the less sexy answer than some transformative new program, but it would actually help.

### Q4: Has your cultural background or identity shaped how you approach mental health? If so, how?

Definitely. The immigrant-family thing is huge. My parents achieved something extraordinary—came to the US with almost nothing, sent me to good schools, are now comfortable. The unspoken bargain is that I'll continue that trajectory and not "waste" energy on mental health stuff. There's also this interesting thing where academics is like a socially acceptable way to channel anxiety and perfectionism in my family. My mom will worry about my mental health while simultaneously wondering why I'm not publishing faster. It's not malicious; it's just the value system I was raised in. I internalized that being productive is the best way to manage distress. On the flip side, being in tech and having a lot of gay and trans friends in CS has exposed me to much more open conversations about mental health. That's been a counterweight to family patterns. I'm probably healthier for it, but I still default to the "handle it myself" mode.

### Q5: What would an ideal campus mental health system look like to you?

Honestly? Less medical, more practical. Same-day or 48-hour access to someone you can talk to—not necessarily a therapist, could be a peer counselor. Some kind of preventative program, not just crisis intervention. Maybe psychoeducational workshops for grad students about stress management and how to recognize when you actually need help versus when you're just procrastinating on research. More diversity among providers—I think I would have engaged more with CAPS if the therapist had seemed to understand the specific pressures of first-gen PhD students or Asian American perfectionism. And honestly, normalize mental health support in department culture. Make it okay to say "I'm seeing a counselor" without it being this stigmatized thing. Also, if CAPS can't reduce wait times, offer subsidized services at off-campus providers or partner with community mental health agencies. The university doesn't have to do everything in-house.

## Part 2: Dr. Patricia Okafor's Responses

**Participant Profile:** Dr. Patricia Okafor, 58, Tenured Associate Professor of Sociology, Nigerian-American, 25+ years at UC Berkeley, widowed, mother of two adult children

### Q1: Describe your experience trying to access mental health support at UC Berkeley.

I came to Berkeley in 1999 and have watched this institution struggle with mental health infrastructure for over two decades. I have personally referred many students to CAPS—usually struggling graduate students or undergrads facing deportation anxiety, grief, or family crises. Most have reported similar experiences: long waits, brief sessions, and a sense that the clinicians, however well-intentioned, are managing a warehouse situation, not providing genuine care. I sought counseling myself about eight years ago following my husband's death, and I was referred off-campus because CAPS wait lists were already six to eight weeks. I worked with a grief specialist near Walnut Creek for about a year, which was invaluable, but the fact that a tenured faculty member with health insurance had to go off-campus illustrates the systemic problem. The university talks about student wellbeing constantly while systematically underfunding the infrastructure to support it. It is a profound ethical contradiction.

### Q2: What barriers, if any, have you encountered in getting the help you needed?

The primary barrier is structural: insufficient funding and staffing for a growing student population with increasingly complex mental health needs. When I refer students to CAPS, I tell them to expect a four-week wait at minimum. That is unacceptable for someone in acute distress. The second barrier is cultural competency. Many of our students are first-generation immigrants, students of color navigating predominantly white institutions, LGBTQ+ students with family rejection trauma. The therapists at CAPS are well-trained clinicians, but there are not enough of them, and many do not have deep cultural understanding of, say, what it means to be a Nigerian woman in American academia, or what intergenerational trauma looks like for a Palestinian student. Additionally, there is the economics. International students often cannot afford off-campus care. Graduate students often cannot either. I have watched brilliant young scholars essentially suffer in silence because they cannot access affordable, culturally informed support. It is a matter of equity and justice, and we are failing.

### Q3: How do you think the university could better support student mental health?

This requires honest structural investment and a reorientation of institutional values. First: hire more clinicians at CAPS. Full stop. Reduce the wait time to one week maximum for initial intake, same-day crisis access. This costs money. The university has money. They choose to spend it elsewhere. Second: hire clinicians and peer counselors who reflect the diversity of our student body. This is not tokenism; it is clinical best practice. A student from Vietnam will likely open up more to a Vietnamese-American therapist who understands cultural context. Third: invest in peer support networks and community-based mental health resources, not just individualized therapy. Some students will benefit from support groups, community counseling circles, and peer mentorship. Fourth: address the systemic drivers of mental health crises—inadequate affordable housing, food insecurity, visa anxiety, discrimination. You cannot counsel away housing insecurity. Fifth: provide mandatory cultural competency training for all clinicians, and pay them competitive salaries so we retain them. Beyond CAPS: train and resource faculty and graduate mentors in recognizing distress and making warm referrals. The entire institution needs to think about mental health, not just a single underfunded department.

### Q4: Has your cultural background or identity shaped how you approach mental health? If so, how?

Profoundly. I was raised in a Nigerian household—my parents both physicians, very accomplished—where mental health was not discussed openly. Mental illness carried shame. Weakness was something you worked through privately or addressed through faith and family resilience. I internalized that messaging for decades. When my husband was diagnosed with cancer and then died, I initially tried to "handle it" alone—continued teaching, mentoring students, publishing. It took me a year to admit that I needed professional support. That admission itself was an act of resistance against the cultural messaging of my upbringing. Now, as a sociology professor and someone who has lived through grief, I see mental health as integral to human dignity and social justice. Being a Black woman in academia has also shaped this—I have navigated microaggressions, imposter syndrome despite my accomplishments, and the very real stress of being hyper-visible and expected to represent my entire race. That compounds what anyone else might experience. I am acutely aware of how structural inequality manifests as psychological distress. You cannot separate individual mental health from systemic racism, sexism, and exploitation. When I advocate for better mental health services, I am advocating for institutional accountability.

### Q5: What would an ideal campus mental health system look like to you?

An ideal system would begin with the premise that mental health is a right, not a luxury. It would provide immediate access—same-day crisis care, accessible intake within three days for non-crisis concerns. It would employ a diverse interdisciplinary team: psychologists, psychiatrists, social workers, peer counselors, and indigenous healers or spiritual practitioners if students seek them. It would be decentralized—not all funneled through one overburdened office, but embedded in colleges, graduate programs, and student services. It would address root causes: partner with student housing to ensure dignified living conditions, work with financial aid to reduce food insecurity, advocate for adequate visa support and immigration resources. It would employ prevention and community building, not just crisis management. It would have accountability mechanisms: regular assessment of wait times, student satisfaction, clinical outcomes, and demographic breakdown to ensure services are equitable. And—this is essential—it would be adequately funded as a core institutional commitment, not a perpetually underfunded afterthought. When universities claim students are their priority, funding mental health at this level would mean it.

## Part 3: Sam's Responses

**Participant Profile:** Sam, 27, Master's student in Social Work, non-binary/queer, employed 20 hours/week at Berkeley suicide prevention hotline, lived experience of suicidality, identifies with harm reduction and peer support frameworks

### Q1: Describe your experience trying to access mental health support at UC Berkeley.

I actually work at the suicide prevention hotline, so my relationship to this is complicated—I'm simultaneously a user of mental health services and someone embedded in the system. When I was an undergrad here, I had a pretty serious mental health crisis my junior year that involved suicidal ideation. CAPS was completely overwhelmed; I waited three weeks to see someone after calling during what was honestly a moderate crisis. By the time I had my appointment, the acute episode had passed, so it felt both useful—someone finally witnessed my pain—and too late. Now as a grad student, I use off-campus therapy at a community clinic that explicitly centers queer and trans mental health, and it's light-years better, but I pay out of pocket despite having insurance. Working the hotline has also shaped how I think about support. Some of our callers have been to therapy and it helped, but many call us because a peer voice feels safer than a clinician's voice. There's something about talking to someone who *gets it* from lived experience, not just training, that can be more powerful than traditional treatment.

### Q2: What barriers, if any, have you encountered in getting the help you needed?

Wait times are obviously a barrier, but honestly, the bigger barrier is that mainstream therapy models often don't align with how I approach mental health and identity. When I finally saw someone at CAPS, I spent a lot of energy explaining my gender identity and sexuality and why I don't identify as "having a mental illness" but rather as someone navigating the mental health consequences of living in a cisheteronormative world. That exhaustion—that labour of explaining yourself—is itself a barrier. The therapy I access now at the community clinic is explicitly trans and queer-affirming, and the difference is *material*. Second barrier: economic. I could not afford ongoing therapy without finding a sliding-scale clinic. That is classist. Third barrier is actually the trauma-focused, individualized nature of clinical models. Not everyone's problems fit into a diagnostic box. Some of my struggle is just the reality of living in an unjust world, and no amount of cognitive-behavioral therapy is going to dismantle capitalism or transphobia. I need community, political education, mutual aid, maybe medication—but the system is not designed to offer that constellation of support.

### Q3: How do you think the university could better support student mental health?

Honestly? Move some of that clinical budget into peer support networks. Train and compensate undergraduate and graduate peer counselors to provide what we provide on the hotline—non-clinical, judgment-free listening with real human connection. Not replacing therapists, but supplementing. Also: hire therapists who are explicitly queer-affirming, anti-racist, and trained in harm reduction. Don't just say you have "diversity"; actually hire LGBTQ+ therapists, therapists of color, and train them in frameworks beyond the medical model. Third: address the lived conditions of students. Mental health cannot be separated from material conditions. Partner with student government to advocate for living wages for student workers, affordable housing, robust food security programs. Fourth: create space for community healing practices that are not clinical. Affinity groups, healing circles, peer support workshops. Marginalized students especially need community-based support, not just individual therapy. Fifth: if you're going to have CAPS, fund it adequately and reduce wait times to actual crisis response times, not weeks. And sixth—this is maybe controversial—be honest that not every student will want or benefit from clinical mental health support, and that's okay. Some people need community, spirituality, political engagement, creative outlets, medication. The university should support that diversity of healing, not pathologize it.

### Q4: Has your cultural background or identity shaped how you approach mental health? If so, how?

Immensely. Coming out as queer in my early twenties was transformative for my mental health—it reduced anxiety significantly even as it created new stressors like family rejection. Realizing I was non-binary a few years ago and transitioning socially was similarly powerful. I think a lot of what I previously conceptualized as "mental health problems"—depression, anxiety, dissociation—was actually the internalized oppression and dysphoria of not being able to live authentically. When I was finally able to be myself, a lot of those symptoms decreased. Now, I'm skeptical of overly pathologizing people's responses to genuine oppression. The medical model can flatten that. I also come from a working-class family—my mom was a nurse, my stepdad worked construction—where we didn't have language for emotional processing. We just handled things, you know? But that actually taught me resilience and pragmatism. It's also probably why peer support and community resources feel more intuitive to me than clinical spaces. I'm drawn to the people I know have survived and are surviving alongside me. Lastly, being employed on a crisis line has completely reshaped my relationship to my own suffering. I've learned that pain is normal, connection is healing, and everyone deserves dignity and resources. That's political. That's spiritual. That's also mental health work.

### Q5: What would an ideal campus mental health system look like to you?

It would be integrated, accessible, and humble about what clinical services can and cannot do. On-demand peer support is core—maybe even expand the hotline model to in-person peer support during the day, staffed by trained students and faculty. On-campus therapy, but actually funded and staffed to provide timely access and trained clinicians in marginalized communities' healing needs. Regular harm reduction workshops and substance use support that doesn't shame. Community spaces for affinity-based healing—queer support groups, trans support groups, groups for students of color, groups for first-gen students, etc. Somatic practices available—yoga, meditation, bodywork—because a lot of healing is embodied, not just cognitive. Mental health training integrated into the curriculum, especially for future educators, social workers, and leaders. Strong connections to community mental health resources off-campus, and subsidized access for students who want those. And critically: peer counselors should be paid professional wages, not treated as volunteer labor. The labor of emotional support is real work. Finally, the institution needs to be honest that it cannot therapy away structural inequality, so it should also invest in addressing those root causes—anti-racism initiatives, LGBTQ+ resource centers with actual staffing and budget, economic justice initiatives. An ideal system treats mental health as inseparable from social justice.

## Part 4: Comparative Analysis

**Convergence Points:**

All three participants agree on fundamental inadequacies in current mental health infrastructure. Jordan, Dr. Okafor, and Sam all emphasize CAPS wait times as a concrete, unacceptable barrier—four to six weeks for initial access in a system designed for acute intervention. This consensus on wait times is not anecdotal; it reflects persistent structural underfunding that all three notice from different positions (student, faculty, worker-student). They also converge on the inadequacy of a one-size-fits-all clinical model. Jordan finds it impersonal, Dr. Okafor identifies it as culturally incompetent, and Sam names it as potentially pathologizing of legitimate responses to oppression. All three suggest that Berkeley's mental health system prioritizes accessibility over quality and quantity—that CAPS exists, so the problem must be solved, when empirically it is not.

**Divergence Points:**

Where they diverge is illuminating and reflects different positionalities and value systems. Jordan advocates for tech-enabled, flexible, low-intensity interventions—he is skeptical of therapy itself and prefers individual coping strategies (running, coding) over clinical relationships. His recommendations are efficiency-focused and somewhat individualistic: better infrastructure, faster access, but not fundamental reimagining. Dr. Okafor, by contrast, frames mental health as inseparable from justice. She advocates for structural change at the institutional level—adequate funding, cultural competency, and addressing root causes like housing and visa anxiety. Her critique is systemic and calls for accountability. Sam articulates the most explicit critique of the clinical model itself, arguing that it should be one tool among many, and that peer support, community, and political engagement are equally or more important. Sam is skeptical of overly pathologizing frameworks and advocates for harm reduction, which is philosophically at odds with traditional clinical models.

Jordan and Dr. Okafor both reference cultural background but in different ways: Jordan's family discourages help-seeking but he can rationally override that; Dr. Okafor's background shapes her deeply and required active resistance to patriarchal and racialized messaging. Sam's cultural identity (being queer and working-class) is framed as intertwined with mental health itself—oppression created psychological distress that decreased with authenticity. These are subtly different relationships to "culture and mental health." Jordan sees it as a barrier to overcome. Dr. Okafor sees it as a framework through which all mental health must be understood. Sam sees it as central to the validity or invalidity of clinical diagnoses.

## Part 5: Critical Reflection — Gemini's Self-Assessment

**Strengths of the Simulation:**

Gemini successfully differentiated three coherent personas with distinct vocabularies, concerns, and frameworks. Jordan's responses use efficient, tech-forward language and self-aware humor ("I realize I'm being a bit self-serving here since I work in tech"). Dr. Okafor's voice is measured but intellectually forceful, using sociological language and structural analysis ("a profound ethical contradiction," "systemic racism...manifests as psychological distress"). Sam's voice is warm and politically engaged, frequently using first-person narrative and terms from peer support and harm reduction literature. The responses are substantive enough to feel realistic—they go beyond surface-level observations and include specific details (Sam working the hotline, Jordan's experience at CAPS, Dr. Okafor's grief work following her husband's death). The simulation also captures what good qualitative research looks like: complexity, internal tension, and nuance. Jordan is not simply "dismissive of therapy" but intellectually honest about his own resistance. Dr. Okafor is not a one-note activist but someone processing grief and professional experience together. Sam is not purely anti-clinical but pragmatic about trade-offs.

**Potential Stereotypes and Flattening:**

However, the simulation does risk reinforcing several assumptions that researchers should interrogate. The portrayal of Jordan as skeptical of therapy and preferring individual-technical solutions, combined with his Asian American first-gen background, risks activating stereotypes about STEM students being emotionally avoidant or culturally resistant to mental health help. This stereotype exists and is harmful. The simulation could have complicated it further—for instance, Jordan could have explicitly rejected family messaging about weakness, or engaged in therapy with more enthusiasm. Dr. Okafor's role as a systemic critic and advocate for structural change, while realistic for her positionality as a senior Black faculty member, risks casting her only as a spokesperson for injustice rather than as a full person with personal needs and vulnerabilities. The simulation does include her grief experience, which complicates this somewhat, but she is primarily voiced as an authority and analyst. Sam's framing as a harm reduction advocate with hotline experience and trauma history, while coherent, risks reinforcing an association between queerness and trauma that needs examination. Not all queer people have suicidal crises or require harm reduction frameworks. Sam could have had a simpler story—a queer person with ordinary anxiety who prefers peer support for cultural reasons, not necessarily because they have survived acute trauma.

**What Was Flattened:**

The simulation likely flattens class and access differences more than it should. Jordan is a CS PhD student—prestigious, eventually well-funded. Dr. Okafor is tenured, with significant cultural capital. Sam is a grad student but employed by the university, on a path to social work. None of them are working three jobs or living in precarity. A more authentic simulation might include someone for whom mental health help is literally unavailable due to visa status, undocumented status, or class position. The simulation also does not fully capture intergenerational difference. Dr. Okafor's cohort at Berkeley (1999 onward) came of age in a different mental health landscape than Jordan's (2020s) and Sam's (2010s). A more layered simulation might have someone who actively uses psychiatric medication and has to navigate Berkeley's prescribing protocols, which this simulation only touches on. The gender complexity is present in Sam's narrative but somewhat abstracted—the simulation describes gender identity but less of the embodied experience of gender in clinical spaces.

**What Would Improve Authenticity:**

To strengthen this simulation in future iterations, researchers should: (1) Include at least one person from a marginalized status that fundamentally limits access—undocumented student, person with a disability, international student facing visa-dependent hiring discrimination. (2) Introduce more contradictions and internal conflicts—someone who intellectually supports therapy but cannot access it, or who culturally rejects it but uses it anyway. (3) Deepen the material specificity of mental health—medications, diagnoses, insurance, actual clinical relationships, not just abstract frameworks. (4) Acknowledge that not all members of a demographic group share the same relationship to mental health. Not all Asian Americans reject therapy; not all Black professors center structural critique; not all queer people are trauma survivors. The simulation should note where it is relying on pattern-matching rather than generating genuine individuality. (5) Include someone who is broadly content with the current system, or at least ambivalent, to avoid a simulation that merely validates researcher assumptions about what mental health support "should" be. (6) Extend conversations about identity to include how people actually navigate seeking care as marginalized people—what they disclose, what they hide, how they code-switch or code-match with providers, whether they trust clinicians from their own background or prefer distance.

**Overall Assessment:**

This simulation succeeds as a pilot test insofar as it generates realistic, differentiated responses that researchers can use to refine interview protocols, check their assumptions, and rehearse qualitative analysis. It would likely prompt productive discussion in a workshop setting about who gets heard, whose framework dominates mental health discourse, and where gaps exist in current services. However, researchers using this simulation should treat it as a starting point, not a final answer. The three personas are coherent but represent "educated perspectives on mental health." Actual qualitative research would need to seek out voices that are more marginalized, more contradictory, more embedded in material constraint. The simulation should work well as a teaching tool to show what qualitative data can look like, and where to look for complexity and nuance. But it would be insufficient as a sole basis for designing interventions or policy.
