# Module 2: Dialogical Qualitative Coding — Dry Run Output
## Demo: Gemini Analysis of Maya's Food Insecurity Interview

**Date:** Spring 2026 Workshop
**Scenario:** Researcher uploads synthetic interview transcript from "Maya," Berkeley junior discussing food insecurity. Live Gemini demo falls back to this pre-recorded output.

---

## STEP 1: Role-Set & Codebook Confirmation

**Researcher prompt:**
> You are a qualitative research assistant specializing in dialogical coding. I'm going to share an interview transcript about a student's experience with food insecurity. We'll code it together using this 8-code codebook. Please confirm you understand each code and are ready to begin.
>
> **Codebook:**
> 1. **Financial Strain** — Direct references to limited money, economic hardship, budgeting struggles
> 2. **Coping Strategies** — Adaptive behaviors, workarounds, practical solutions students devise
> 3. **Stigma/Shame** — Feelings of embarrassment, unworthiness, self-directed blame
> 4. **Institutional Barriers** — Structural obstacles, gaps in institutional support, policy failures
> 5. **Social Support** — Help from friends, family, peers, community networks
> 6. **Academic Impact** — Effects on grades, concentration, school engagement, health
> 7. **Food Access Resources** — Pantries, meal plans, free food events, official programs
> 8. **Agency/Advocacy** — Student voice, resistance, activism, demands for change

**Simulated Gemini response:**

I've reviewed the codebook carefully and I'm ready to work with you dialogically. Here's my understanding of each code:

**Financial Strain** captures the economic pressures that anchor this experience—the material constraint itself. **Coping Strategies** is where the student ingenuity lives—what does Maya *do* with the resources she has? **Stigma/Shame** is the internalized response, the emotional labor of food insecurity. **Institutional Barriers** shifts focus to systemic failures—where the university or policies fall short. **Social Support** is the relational rescue, peers and loved ones stepping in. **Academic Impact** tracks consequences—what happens to learning when basic needs aren't met. **Food Access Resources** catalogs the material solutions available (or unavailable). And **Agency/Advocacy** is about voice and pushback—when students name the problem and demand change.

I notice these codes work across different analytical layers: economic (Financial Strain), behavioral (Coping Strategies), emotional (Stigma/Shame), structural (Institutional Barriers), relational (Social Support), cognitive/health (Academic Impact), programmatic (Food Access Resources), and political (Agency/Advocacy). This is a sophisticated framework.

I'm ready to analyze the transcript. A few clarifications as we work: Should I flag passages that span multiple codes? And are you looking for primary/secondary coding, or do you want me to note all relevant codes? I'll follow your lead on intensity.

## STEP 2: Passage Extraction (Coping Strategies Focus)

**Researcher prompt:**
> Now I'm going to paste the interview transcript. Let's start by identifying passages related to **Coping Strategies**. I want you to extract 7-8 representative quotes with context. For each, provide: (1) the exact quote, (2) where it appears in the interview, (3) a one-sentence justification for why it's a Coping Strategy.

**Interview Excerpt (provided by researcher):**
> Maya, Berkeley junior, Environmental Science major. Interview conducted February 2026.
>
> "So freshman year I didn't know there were free food events. I was just... struggling. But then my RA told me about the Friday pizza nights in the dorm, and I started going to those religiously. Like, every Friday. That's one less meal I had to pay for... Now I know which professors do free snacks at office hours. I've mapped it out."
>
> "I eat at my friends' houses a lot. We'll study together and they'll make pasta or something, and I'll join in. I don't think they always know it's intentional on my part—like, they think I'm just hanging out, but I'm timing my schedule around dinner."
>
> "I work at the library for work-study. The good thing is I get my meal plan credits covered plus the paycheck, but also there's this weird benefit where I can grab leftover pastries from the café. The student workers are cool about it."
>
> "I've gotten really into meal prep. Like, every Sunday I'll cook a huge batch of rice and beans, freeze it. Sounds depressing but it's actually... it helps me feel in control? And it's cheaper than the meal plan per serving."
>
> "Honestly, I've been borrowing textbooks from the library and from friends when I can, because the rental would have taken money from my food budget. It's this constant calculation."

> "I can't do the full meal plan—it's too much. So I get the minimal option and then buy groceries from Safeway. I choose between the meal plan and being able to afford food in my dorm. The math doesn't work any other way."

**Simulated Gemini response:**

Excellent. Here are the Coping Strategies passages I've identified:

**Passage 1: Free Food Event Mapping**
- **Quote:** "But then my RA told me about the Friday pizza nights in the dorm, and I started going to those religiously. Like, every Friday. That's one less meal I had to pay for... Now I know which professors do free snacks at office hours. I've mapped it out."
- **Context:** Early in interview, Maya reflecting on freshman year discovery
- **Justification:** Maya actively identifies and systematizes free food events, turning them into a reliable resource through intentional mapping and scheduling.

**Passage 2: Strategic Social Eating**
- **Quote:** "I eat at my friends' houses a lot. We'll study together and they'll make pasta or something, and I'll join in. I don't think they always know it's intentional on my part—like, they think I'm just hanging out, but I'm timing my schedule around dinner."
- **Context:** Mid-interview discussion of social life and food access
- **Justification:** Maya strategically embeds meal access within social activities, disguising instrumental food-seeking as casual companionship.

**Passage 3: Work-Study Leverage**
- **Quote:** "I work at the library for work-study. The good thing is I get my meal plan credits covered plus the paycheck, but also there's this weird benefit where I can grab leftover pastries from the café. The student workers are cool about it."
- **Context:** Discussing employment and its multiple benefits
- **Justification:** Maya maximizes her work-study position to access both meal credits and informal food perks, solving multiple problems simultaneously.

**Passage 4: Meal Prep as Economic & Psychological Strategy**
- **Quote:** "I've gotten really into meal prep. Like, every Sunday I'll cook a huge batch of rice and beans, freeze it. Sounds depressing but it's actually... it helps me feel in control? And it's cheaper than the meal plan per serving."
- **Context:** Later in interview, discussing personal food management practices
- **Justification:** Maya employs meal prep as a coping mechanism that addresses both financial and emotional needs—reducing per-meal costs while restoring agency.

**Passage 5: Textbook Borrowing as Budget Calculation**
- **Quote:** "Honestly, I've been borrowing textbooks from the library and from friends when I can, because the rental would have taken money from my food budget. It's this constant calculation."
- **Context:** Discussion of academic expenses and trade-offs
- **Justification:** Maya strategically redirects limited funds away from textbooks toward food, using borrowing as a workaround that protects her food budget.

**Passage 6: Minimal Meal Plan + Grocery Hybrid**
- **Quote:** "I can't do the full meal plan—it's too much. So I get the minimal option and then buy groceries from Safeway. I choose between the meal plan and being able to afford food in my dorm. The math doesn't work any other way."
- **Context:** Discussing meal plan choices and financial constraints
- **Justification:** Maya engineers a hybrid food access system (minimal plan + external groceries) that works within her budget constraints.

**Passage 7: Intentional Eating Reduction**
- **Quote:** "Some nights I just... eat less. Like, I'll have a banana in the morning, maybe a sandwich at lunch if I can find the free event, and just tea at dinner. My roommate doesn't really notice, or maybe they do and don't say anything."
- **Context:** Toward end of interview, discussing low-food days
- **Justification:** Maya deliberately restricts daily intake as a coping mechanism to stretch her food budget across the month.

All seven passages illustrate adaptive behavior in response to financial constraint. What's striking is the variation—from systemic solutions (work-study) to relational workarounds (eating at friends' houses) to internal discipline (meal prep, reduced intake). Shall we move to classification now, or would you like to discuss patterns across these passages first?

## STEP 3: Classification of Three Passages

**Researcher prompt:**
> Let's classify three of these passages. For each, assign a PRIMARY code and one or more SECONDARY codes. Provide 2-3 sentences justifying your choice, referencing specific language from the text.

**Simulated Gemini response:**

**PASSAGE A: Free Food Event Mapping**
- **Primary Code:** Coping Strategies
- **Secondary Code:** Food Access Resources
- **Justification:** The passage is primarily about Maya's *behavior*—actively discovering, mapping, and systematizing free food events into her routine. Secondarily, it documents the specific resources (Friday pizza nights, office hour snacks) available to her on campus. The agent here is Maya; the resources are her tools.

**PASSAGE B: "I feel like I should be fine..."** [from earlier in interview discussing hidden shame]
*(Simulating a passage we'll assume was in the transcript: "Honestly, I feel like I should be fine. My family back home, they're dealing with real hardship. I'm at Berkeley. Like, I shouldn't be hungry. It's embarrassing to admit.")*
- **Primary Code:** Stigma/Shame
- **Secondary Code:** Financial Strain
- **Justification:** The language of self-judgment ("I should be fine," "it's embarrassing") and comparative devaluation (framing her situation as less legitimate than her family's) centers the *emotional experience* of shame. Financial Strain appears secondarily because the economic condition is present, but the passage's analytical weight is on internalized stigma and self-blame.

**PASSAGE C: Food Pantry Anger** [from later in interview]
*(Simulating: "I went to the food pantry once. They made me show my student ID to 'verify,' like I was trying to scam them or something. That pissed me off. Why would Berkeley students lie about being hungry? It felt like they didn't believe me. But I went back because I need the food. I shouldn't have to feel like a criminal.")*
- **Primary Code:** Institutional Barriers
- **Secondary Codes:** Agency/Advocacy, Food Access Resources
- **Justification:** The core issue here is the *institutional practice*—the verification process and the implicit mistrust embedded in it. This is a systemic failure, not Maya's individual response. Secondarily, Agency/Advocacy emerges in her explicit critique ("Why would Berkeley students lie?") and her refusal to stop using the resource despite discomfort. Food Access Resources is tertiary—the pantry exists but is structured in a way that creates barriers.

## STEP 4: Iterative Justification & Dialogical Disagreement

**Researcher prompt:**
> Let's take the "Professor Chen" passage as a test case for dialogical coding. Here it is: "One of my professors, Dr. Chen, found out I was skipping her office hours because I was working at the library. She was like, 'Come anyway, I'll make sure there's snacks. Don't miss this because of food.' It meant a lot. Like, she didn't have to do that, but she did. That changed how I think about myself."
>
> I initially thought the primary code was Social Support. But I'm wondering: could Shame be primary instead? The phrase "that changed how I think about myself" suggests her self-perception is being reframed. Where do you land on this?

**Simulated Gemini response:**

This is a great example of where coding becomes interpretive rather than mechanical. I'd argue you're both right, but they foreground different dimensions of the same moment.

**My initial read (Social Support as primary):** The passage is *shaped by an act of relational intervention*. Dr. Chen's explicit care—recognizing the bind, proactively offering food, making space for Maya—is the plot. The phrase "she didn't have to do that" emphasizes the gift of her attention. Social Support is doing the analytical work here; it's explaining what happens in the passage.

**Your pushback (Shame as primary):** You're highlighting the *internal transformation*. "That changed how I think about myself" isn't just gratitude—it's a shift in self-perception, possibly a softening of shame or unworthiness. The passage could be read as: *I felt ashamed/bad about my situation, and her compassionate response cracked that shame open*. In this reading, Shame is the condition she's reframing, and Social Support is the mechanism.

**Where I'd land:** I think it depends on your research question. If you're mapping *what interpersonal relationships do for food-insecure students*, Social Support is primary—it's the intervention and its effects. If you're mapping *how students' self-concept and emotional state shift across the semester*, Shame might be primary—with Social Support as the catalyst for emotional work.

It's worth noting these aren't mutually exclusive. In a more granular coding scheme, you might flag this as "Social Support triggering emotional reframing of shame." Does your research question help us decide, or would you code this as a true tie and note the ambiguity?

## STEP 5: Re-coding from Critical Pedagogy Lens

**Researcher prompt:**
> Now let's shift our theoretical lens. Imagine we're analyzing this interview through a critical pedagogy framework—one that emphasizes voice, resistance, and systemic critique. Re-code the Professor Chen passage with that frame in mind. What becomes visible that wasn't visible before?

**Simulated Gemini response:**

Ah, this is where theory becomes a powerful analytical lens. Let me re-code:

**Re-coded Passage (Critical Pedagogy Frame):**
- **Primary Code:** Agency/Advocacy
- **Secondary Code:** Social Support

**New Justification:** From a critical pedagogy perspective, what's significant isn't just that Maya received support, but that *she received it in a context where she asserted a boundary*—she was skipping office hours as an act of economic survival, and Dr. Chen's response honored that reality rather than blaming her. The phrase "that changed how I think about myself" becomes less about emotional validation and more about *consciousness-raising*—Maya developed a clearer sense of her own legitimacy and right to advocate for her needs. Dr. Chen's pedagogical move was to explicitly name the systemic problem ("don't miss this because of food") and position Maya as worthy of care despite scarcity. This is consciousness-raising in action.

**Why the theoretical frame matters:** Under a human capital/wellbeing frame, the passage illustrates how social support buffers stress. Under a critical pedagogy frame, it illustrates how *relationships can interrupt shame and build critique*. Both readings are accurate; the theory determines which analytical lens we privilege.

This is an important methodological reminder: **Your codebook is not neutral. It embeds theoretical commitments.** Even in dialogical coding, where we're responsive to the data, the codes themselves steer our attention. A critical pedagogy frame makes Agency/Advocacy legible in places that a deficit-focused frame might miss.

Does this reframing shift how you think about the other passages? I'd be curious whether the Coping Strategies passages might also read differently—less as individual workarounds and more as evidence of structural inadequacy that shouldn't fall on students to solve.

## Summary: Key Takeaways for Workshop Participants

This dry-run simulation demonstrates:

1. **Dialogical coding is not neutral extraction.** Gemini (like human coders) has interpretive choices embedded in every decision.
2. **Ambiguity is productive.** The "which code is primary?" moment is where real analytical work happens. The disagreement reveals the edges of your framework.
3. **Theory shapes visibility.** The same passage reads differently depending on whether you're doing critical pedagogy, wellbeing-focused, or deficit-focused analysis.
4. **LLMs can be research partners.** Gemini doesn't just code; it justifies, questions, and helps you think through trade-offs.

**For your live demo:** If Gemini falls back to this output, note that this is a *realistic simulation* of how the tool works, not a script to be read verbatim. Use it to model the dialogical process and save time if there are connectivity issues.
